The PG&E Union Retirement Benefits Guide

What a new advisor needs to read, explain, and plan around for Pacific Gas and Electric Company's union-represented employees: the pension, the 401(k), and the Retiree Medical Savings Account, plus the decisions that matter most for clients.

Section 01

The benefit landscape

A retiring PG&E union employee can draw income and support from up to four sources. Understanding how they fit together helps you frame the whole picture in a first meeting.

🏛️

The Pension

An employer-funded benefit from the PG&E Retirement Plan. Paid as a monthly annuity (or, for Cash Balance, optionally a lump sum).

💼

401(k) / RSP

The Retirement Savings Plan, held at Fidelity. Employee and employer money, investable and inheritable. Grows with the market.

⚕️

RMSA

The Retiree Medical Savings Account. A separate PG&E-funded account that reimburses part of retiree medical premiums. Unique to PG&E; not a typical benefit.

🇺🇸

Social Security

The federal layer. Not a PG&E benefit, but it shapes claiming timing and the income gap a client is solving for.

The advisor's lens

Think of these as playing different roles. The pension is the guaranteed-income floor. The 401(k) is the flexible, growth asset. The RMSA is a dedicated healthcare-cost bucket that only pays for medical premiums, it can't be spent on anything else or left to heirs. A simple framing for clients: pension pays the bills, 401(k) builds the future, RMSA covers the health insurance gap until Medicare.

Accuracy first

The figures and tables in this guide come from the PG&E Summary Plan Description (SPD) library available for this project, along with current IRS, Social Security, and Medicare figures. Plan mechanics can change with newer Summaries of Material Modification (SMMs) this project did not have access to. Always verify a client's actual numbers through the PG&E Pension Center or Fidelity NetBenefits before giving advice. This guide builds intuition; it is not a substitute for the controlling plan documents.

Section 02

Eligibility & vesting

Two questions decide whether a client has a benefit at all: are they covered, and are they vested?

Who is covered

This guide applies to full-time, part-time, and probationary union-represented employees covered by a collective bargaining agreement with PG&E — specifically IBEW, ESC, and SEIU 383 members. Participation in the Retirement Plan generally begins on the employee's first day of work; there is no waiting period to start earning a benefit.

Not eligible

Temporary employees not expected to become regular employees, Management/Administrative & Technical (A&T) employees, contract, agency, or hiring hall employees, and retirees of the Company. Hiring hall, outage, temporary additional, and intermittent IBEW employees (who have not attained regular status) also don't participate.

How vesting works

Vesting is the point where the benefit belongs to the employee for good, even if they leave PG&E before retirement age. For the pension, the threshold depends on which formula applies; the 401(k) works differently:

Benefit typeVesting requirement
Cash Balance Pension (hired on/after Jan 1, 2013)3 years of service, or reaching age 55 while employed
Final Pay Pension (hired before Jan 1, 2013)5 years of service, or reaching age 55 while employed
401(k) / Retirement Savings Plan (RSP)Immediate: all employee and employer contributions are 100% vested right away (see Section 09)

If a client earns a benefit under both formulas (see Section 03), 3 years of combined service vests both. No employee contributions are required for either formula, PG&E pays the full cost.

Client example

Maria Delgado, an IBEW-represented line worker, was hired by PG&E in March 2008. By March 2013 she had already completed 5 years of service, so she was fully vested in her Final Pay Pension years before the 2013 Cash Balance election came along. If Maria were laid off tomorrow, that vested benefit is hers to collect starting at 55, PG&E can't take it away. We'll follow Maria's numbers through the rest of this guide.

The advisor's lens

A client who is not yet vested and thinking about leaving PG&E is making a bigger decision than they may realize, walking away early can mean forfeiting the pension entirely. If a client is close to a vesting milestone, that timing belongs in the conversation.

Section 03 · The Pension

Two formulas, one hire date

This is the first thing to figure out for any client. PG&E's Retirement Plan has two completely different pension formulas, and which one applies is decided almost entirely by hire date.

Final Pay Pension

Hired before January 1, 2013

A traditional formula: your pay near retirement × years of service. PG&E pays the full cost. Can be reduced for early retirement (Section 05).

Cash Balance Pension

Hired on or after January 1, 2013

A hypothetical account that grows with pay-based credits plus interest. Feels like a savings account, but is legally a pension. Payable at any age once vested, though a lump sum taken before separation at 55 (or age 59½) may trigger the IRS 10% early-distribution penalty unless rolled over.

The key question

Ask every client: "What year were you hired at PG&E?" and "Were you employed on December 31, 2012?" Employees hired before 2013 were automatically enrolled in the Final Pay Pension. PG&E offered those employees a one-time election in 2013 to switch to the Cash Balance Pension instead. If a client switched, their Final Pay benefit was frozen as of December 31, 2013 (based on pay and service through that date), and their Cash Balance account started growing from January 1, 2014 onward, meaning some long-tenured clients have both a small frozen Final Pay benefit and a growing Cash Balance account.

Clients can check which formula applies, and run a real pension estimate ("Estimate My Pension Benefit"), at PG&E PensionConnect: mypensionconnect.com.

Client example

Maria Delgado was hired in 2008, before the cutoff, and did not make the 2013 election, so she has a Final Pay Pension only. Compare that to Devon Park, hired in 2016: because he started after the cutoff, he has a Cash Balance Pension only. Same employer, same union, two entirely different pension formulas, purely because of when they were hired.

Section 04

The Final Pay Pension

Applies to union employees hired before January 1, 2013 (and who did not switch to Cash Balance in the 2013 election). It's a classic defined-benefit formula: PG&E pays the full cost, and the employee makes no contributions.

How the benefit is calculated

The formula runs in three steps:

Step 1Straight-time hourly rate × 2,080 hrs ÷ 52 wks, rounded up to nearest $10
→
Step 2× 52 ÷ 12 = Monthly Equivalent Pay
→
Step 3× (1.5% × years up to 25) + (1.6% × years over 25)

The pay rate used is the employee's straight-time hourly rate as of 30 days before retirement. There is no employee contribution and no cap on years of credited service; the benefit is payable as a lifetime monthly annuity.

Worked example (from the SPD)

An employee retires at age 65 with 30 years of credited service. Their straight-time hourly rate as of the calculation date is $34.62, so Basic Weekly Pay = $34.62 × 2,080 ÷ 52 = $1,384.80, rounded up to the nearest $10 = $1,390.00. Monthly Equivalent Pay = $1,390.00 × 52 ÷ 12 = $6,023.33.

Monthly Pension Benefit = $6,023.33 × [(1.5% × 25) + (1.6% × 5)] = $6,023.33 × 0.455 = $2,740.62 per month, payable for life as a single life annuity.

Client example: Maria Delgado

Maria plans to retire at 65 with 28 years of credited service. Her straight-time hourly rate 30 days before retirement is $53.00, so her Basic Weekly Pay = $53.00 × 2,080 ÷ 52 = $2,120.00, and her Monthly Equivalent Pay = $2,120.00 × 52 ÷ 12 = $9,186.67.

Her formula percentage at 28 years = (1.5% × 25) + (1.6% × 3) = 42.3%. Monthly Pension Benefit = $9,186.67 × 0.423 = $3,885.96/month at normal retirement, unreduced.

Normal retirement is age 65 (unreduced). Vesting is 100% at 5 years of service or age 55 while employed, whichever comes first (3 years if the employee is also earning a Cash Balance benefit).

The advisor's lens

Because the formula uses pay near retirement, not a career average, a late-career raise or promotion has an outsized effect on this pension. It's worth asking whether a client's current pay reflects their long-term rate, e.g., someone temporarily demoted or on leave near retirement may have special pay rules apply (see the SPD's "Basic Weekly Pay" provisions).

Section 05

Early retirement & the reduction table

This is where advisors add the most value on the Final Pay Pension. Retiring before age 65 usually cuts the monthly benefit, unless the client has enough service to avoid it entirely.

Early retirement is available starting at age 55 (there's no minimum service requirement to retire early, just to avoid the reduction). Two rules matter most:

The 30-year exemption

A client with 30 or more years of credited service gets their full, unreduced Final Pay Pension at any retirement age from 55 to 65. This is the single biggest lever in the whole formula, worth checking for every long-tenured client.

Client example: Maria Delgado

Suppose Maria, instead of working to 65 (Section 04's example), retires early at 58 with 22 years of service, using the same $9,186.67 Monthly Equivalent Pay. Two things shrink her benefit at once:

Smaller accrual: her formula percentage is only (1.5% × 22) = 33.0%, not 42.3%, since she has fewer years. Full accrued benefit = $9,186.67 × 0.33 = $3,031.60/month.

Plus the reduction: at age 58 with 22 years (the "15–24 yrs" column), the table above shows a 14% cut. $3,031.60 × (1 − 0.14) = $2,607.18/month, versus $3,885.96/month if she waits to 65, a difference of nearly $1,300/month for retiring 7 years early.

The advisor's lens

Notice that with 20 years of service, the reduction disappears entirely by age 62, not 65. The full 10-year reduction window (55→65) only applies to clients with fewer than 15 years of service. Always look up the client's exact service band; assuming "early retirement always means a reduction all the way to 65" understates the benefit for most mid-career clients.

Section 06

Cash Balance mechanics

The Cash Balance Pension applies to employees hired on or after January 1, 2013 (and to anyone who elected it during the 2013 one-time choice window). Instead of a final-pay formula, the employee accumulates a hypothetical account balance that grows every year through pay credits and every quarter through interest credits.

Pay credits: based on "points"

Once a year (December 31), the account is credited with a percentage of that year's covered pay. The percentage depends on points, age plus years of service, both counted in whole numbers:

Points (age + years of service)Annual pay credit
Fewer than 40 points5% of pay
40–49 points6% of pay
50–59 points7% of pay
60–69 points8% of pay
70–79 points9% of pay
80 or more points10% of pay
Worked example

An employee has worked at PG&E for 7 years and 9 months and is age 55½. Using whole numbers only: 7 years of service + 55 years of age = 62 points → the 60–69 band → an 8% pay credit that year.

Interest credits

Each calendar quarter, the account also earns interest on its balance. The rate is the average of the 30-year U.S. Treasury bond yield for the three months before that quarter (published by the IRS), with a floor of 0.4875% per quarter (about 1.95% per year). See irs.gov: weighted average interest rate table for the published monthly rates.

How an account grows over time

Example: an employee is age 45 with 10 years of service, earning $90,000/year (raises of 3%/year). Starting from a $0 balance:

YearPointsPay credit rateEnding balance
1557%$6,300
3597%$20,058
5638%$37,503

The pay credit rate steps up as points cross each band, and interest compounds on the growing balance, so the account accelerates over time.

Enhanced 401(k) match

Employees earning a Cash Balance Pension are eligible for a richer employer 401(k) match than Final Pay Pension participants, $0.75 per dollar contributed, up to 8% of pay. See Section 09.

Vesting & payment

Vested after 3 years of service or age 55 while employed, whichever comes first. Unlike the Final Pay Pension, there is no early-retirement reduction, the account can be paid out at any age once the employee leaves PG&E, either as a one-time lump sum (rollable to an IRA) or as a monthly annuity for life.

Note: a lump sum paid directly to the employee rather than rolled into an IRA or other qualified plan is subject to the IRS early-withdrawal rules, generally a 10% early-distribution penalty (on top of ordinary income tax and the 20% mandatory withholding) if distributed before age 59½, unless an exception applies, such as separating from PG&E in or after the year the employee turns 55. See the Devon Park example below.

Client example: Devon Park

Devon, hired in 2016, is now 34 with 10 years of service: 34 + 10 = 44 points, landing in the 40–49 band for a 6% pay credit. On his $85,000 covered pay this year, that's $85,000 × 6% = $5,100 added to his account. Assume his account entered the year at $28,000; this year's interest credit (illustrative 4.5% annual rate, check the IRS table above for the current published figure) adds $28,000 × 4.5% = $1,260. Ending balance: $28,000 + $1,260 (interest) + $5,100 (pay credit) = $34,360.

Client example: Devon Park

Fast-forward: suppose Devon leaves PG&E and elects a lump sum at age 54, paid directly to him rather than rolled into an IRA, once his account has grown to an assumed $150,000. Per the SPD's Tax Considerations, PG&E must withhold 20% federal tax ($30,000) from the distribution, so Devon receives $120,000 up front.

Because Devon is separating from PG&E before the year he turns 55, and he didn't roll the money over, none of the IRS's exceptions apply (age-55 separation, disability, substantially equal life-expectancy payments, or death). At tax time, the distribution is also hit with the IRS's additional 10% early-distribution penalty (≈$15,000), on top of his regular income tax, a combined tax drag of roughly 30% of the account balance before ordinary income tax is even factored in. Waiting until the year he turns 55 to separate, or rolling the lump sum directly into an IRA instead, avoids the 10% penalty entirely.

Section 08

How the pension gets paid

At retirement, the client chooses a form of payment. Once payments begin, the choice is generally irrevocable, so it's worth getting right the first time. (Payment elections under the Final Pay Pension and Cash Balance Pension are made separately, a client with both may choose differently for each.)

Single Life Pension

Covers one lifetime
  • Highest monthly payment
  • Stops at death, nothing continues to a survivor
  • If married, requires notarized spousal consent to elect

Joint Pension

25% / 50% / 75% / 100%
  • Continues to a named "joint pensioner" (spouse or anyone else) after death
  • Default for married employees is 50% to spouse
  • Lower monthly payment; the more continued and the younger the joint pensioner, the bigger the reduction

Special Joint Pension ("Pop-Up")

PG&E-specific feature
  • If the joint pensioner dies first, the benefit "pops up" to the full single-life amount
  • Costs more upfront than a regular joint pension (further reduced to pay for the pop-up feature)
Defaults & consent

If the client is married when payments begin, federal law requires the default to be at least a 50% joint pension with the spouse (a "Marital Pension"). Electing anything else, including single life or a joint pension with someone other than the spouse, requires the spouse's written, notarized consent. If unmarried, the default is single life.

Worked example: 50% joint pension (from the SPD)

Employee is age 65; joint pensioner (spouse) is age 62. Basic monthly pension is $1,655.40. Because the joint pensioner is younger and likely to receive payments longer, an actuarial factor reduces the benefit:

Amount
Reduced monthly pension (factor 0.947)$1,655.40 × 0.947 = $1,567.66
Survivor benefit after employee's death (50%)$1,567.66 × 50% = $783.83

The "Pop-Up" version of this election would cost a bit more than $1,567.66 up front, but if the spouse dies before the employee, the employee's payment would jump back up to the full $1,655.40 for the rest of their life.

Client example: Maria Delgado

Maria retires at 65 with the $3,885.96/month benefit from Section 04. Her husband is also 62, the same ages as the SPD example above, so the same 0.947 factor applies to her own numbers: $3,885.96 × 0.947 = $3,680.13/month to Maria, and if she predeceases him, he'd continue receiving 50% of that, $1,840.07/month, for the rest of his life. Electing Single Life instead would pay Maria the full $3,885.96, but nothing would continue to her husband.

The advisor's lens

The Pop-Up option is easy to overlook but genuinely useful: it removes the biggest fear clients have about joint pensions, "what if my spouse dies first and I'm stuck with a permanently reduced check?" Model the extra up-front cost against that peace of mind, especially for clients with a meaningful age gap or health differences between spouses.

Mandatory cash-out for small benefits

If the present value of a vested pension is $5,000 or less, it is paid as a lump sum regardless of formula. If the client doesn't return completed paperwork within 60 days: a value over $1,000 up to $5,000 is automatically rolled into an IRA PG&E selects; $1,000 or less is cashed out. (PG&E's current materials keep this threshold at $5,000, it did not adopt the higher $7,000 ceiling that SECURE 2.0 permits.)

Watch for: the Excess Plan (high earners)

Clients whose pension would exceed the IRS annual limits (IRC §415 benefit limit and §401(a)(17) pay limit) have the excess paid through PG&E's separate Excess Plan. Two things to flag: it's paid only as a lump sum at retirement (a projected Excess Plan benefit can shrink or vanish with future pay or IRS-limit changes), and it's a non-qualified benefit, meaning it's unsecured and could be lost if PG&E were to go bankrupt (not a hypothetical worry for this employer). Not PBGC-insured. More likely to surface with heavy-overtime, long-tenure union clients.

Section 09 · The Savings Plan

The 401(k) / Retirement Savings Plan

PG&E Corporation's Retirement Savings Plan (RSP) is the company's 401(k), held at Fidelity Investments. Effective January 1, 2026, union-represented employees may contribute 1%–50% of eligible pay (raised from the prior 20% cap), in 1% increments, subject to IRS limits.

New for 2026: the Roth 401(k) source

Starting January 1, 2026, the RSP adds a Roth contribution option, a genuinely new feature (earlier plan documents did not offer one). Clients can now split contributions across three sources: pre-tax, Roth (after-tax dollars in, tax-free qualified growth and withdrawals out), and regular after-tax (the mega-backdoor bucket). Any mix is allowed, as long as total contributions stay within the IRS annual limits.

This also satisfies a SECURE 2.0 mandate that kicks in the same year: for anyone who earned more than $150,000 in the prior year, age-50+ catch-up contributions must be made as Roth. With the Roth source now live, those clients can comply directly in the plan.

The advisor's lens: Roth changes the career-stage conversation

Before 2026, a union client wanting tax-free retirement dollars had to route through the after-tax "mega-backdoor" bucket and convert. Now they can simply elect Roth directly. Younger or lower-bracket clients who expect higher future tax rates are the classic Roth candidates; higher-bracket clients late in their career may still prefer pre-tax. Tax diversification, holding some of each, gives them flexibility to manage taxable income in retirement (see IRMAA, Section 11).

Employer match: two different formulas

The match a client receives depends on which pension formula they're earning:

Final Pay Pension participants

1–3 years of service: 60% match on contributions up to 3% of pay.
3+ years of service: 60% match on contributions up to 6% of pay.

Cash Balance Pension participants

Enhanced match: $0.75 per dollar contributed, up to 8% of pay, once the employee has completed 1 year of service.

Worked example

A Cash Balance participant earning $90,000/year contributes 8% of pay: $7,200/year. The employer match is $0.75 × $7,200 = $5,400/year, an immediate, guaranteed 75% return on that contribution. Contributing less than 8% leaves part of that match unclaimed.

Client example: Maria Delgado

Maria is a Final Pay Pension participant (28 years of service, well past the 3-year mark) earning roughly $110,000/year. To get the full 60% match, she needs to contribute 6% of pay: $110,000 × 6% = $6,600/year. Her employer match = 60% × $6,600 = $3,960/year. If she only contributes 4%, she'd still get 60% of that smaller amount, leaving 2% of match on the table for good, that pay period's match isn't made up later.

Employer matching contributions are invested in the PG&E Corporation Stock Fund by default but can be reallocated at any time. All employee and employer contributions are 100% vested immediately.

The "spillover" strategy

Once a client maxes out the $24,500 pre-tax deferral limit for the year, the SPD's spillover election automatically shifts further payroll contributions to after-tax dollars, so contributions (and the employer match on them, up to the plan's matched cap) can continue for the rest of the year instead of stopping. After-tax dollars can later be rolled to a Roth IRA (a "mega-backdoor Roth"), converting a large after-tax balance into tax-free growth.

Confirm before recommending

The spillover election, and whether after-tax dollars can be converted to Roth in-plan or via in-service rollover, should be confirmed in NetBenefits or with Fidelity before building a strategy around it.

Investing & accessing the account

The RSP offers three tiers: Tier 1 — Target Date Funds (11 funds; the default if no election is made, closest to the employee's 65th birthday, or the RSP Retirement Income Fund at 65+), Tier 2 — Core Funds (7 pre-screened, mostly low-fee index funds), and Tier 3 — a Self-Directed Account (Fidelity BrokerageLink, expanded on August 1, 2024 to include ETFs, ETNs, and closed-end funds). A separate 20% cap limits how much of an account can be steered into the PG&E Corporation Stock Fund. Loans are available up to the lesser of $50,000 or 50% of the vested Tier 1/Tier 2 balance.

Investment advice for clients

PG&E offers free online advice and fee-based professional management through Edelman Financial Engines (EdelmanFinancialEngines.com/forpge). Clients age 60+ or within five years of retirement can add the Income+ service, which can turn the 401(k) balance into managed monthly payouts. Useful to know so you can position your own advice relative to what PG&E already provides.

Section 10

Retiree Medical Savings Account (RMSA)

This is one of PG&E's most distinctive benefits, a dedicated account that helps pay retiree medical premiums. It is not a cash account: it has no cash value, can't be used for anything but medical premiums, and is forfeited if unused at death.

Eligibility

To receive RMSA benefits, an employee generally must:

  • Attain age 55 before terminating or retiring from PG&E, and
  • Have at least 10 years of credited service under the Retirement Plan (for employees who terminate on or after January 1, 2004).

A client who leaves PG&E before age 55, even with a fully vested pension, is not eligible for RMSA or retiree medical coverage at all. This is a hard gate worth flagging early.

How the account grows

The RMSA is funded entirely by PG&E, no employee contributions, and grows through several pieces stacked together:

Every year from age 45$5,000 credit
+
At retirement$1,000 × years of service beyond 15
+
At retirementUp to $7,500 lump sum (10–25 yrs, prorated)
+
From age 464.5% interest, compounded annually
A separate account for spouses, too

A married employee's spouse or registered domestic partner gets a parallel RMSA that grows the same way, $5,000 for every year the employee works at PG&E starting the year the employee turns 45, plus 4.5% interest starting the year the employee turns 46. The spouse's account does not get the $1,000/year service bonus or the $7,500 lump sum; those are employee-only. In a household where both spouses work at PG&E, only one RMSA per employee is allowed, a retiree RMSA or a spousal RMSA, not both.

Worked example (condensed from the SPD)

An employee hired at age 30 works continuously and retires at age 62, with 32 years of service. From age 45 through retirement (18 years), the account receives $5,000/year plus 4.5% compounding interest starting at 46, then at retirement adds a $7,500 lump sum (25+ years of service) and $17,000 (17 years beyond the 15-year threshold × $1,000). In the SPD's example using these mechanics, the account reaches roughly $150,000–$155,000 by retirement.

Client example: Maria Delgado

Maria retires at 60 with 25 years of service, having earned RMSA credits since turning 45 (15 years of $5,000 credits): $5,000 × 15 = $75,000. At retirement, she also gets a service bonus of $1,000 × (25 − 15) = $10,000, plus the maximum $7,500 lump sum (she's at the top of the 10–25 year proration range). That's $92,500 before any interest, and 4.5% annual compounding since age 46 pushes her actual balance meaningfully higher, the same way it took Joe's example above from simple credits to $153,208.55.

Coverage stageRMSA pays
Pre-Medicare (under 65)55% of the monthly premium
Medicare-eligible (65+)30% of the monthly premium

The retiree pays the remaining percentage out of pocket. Once the account is fully depleted, the retiree pays 100% of the premium. Any RMSA credits PG&E contributes are not taxed as income to the employee.

The advisor's lens

The RMSA rewards staying longer, both in years of service (the $1,000/year and $7,500 bonuses) and in delaying the start of interest crediting (which only begins at 46). When a client is weighing an earlier retirement against the Final Pay Pension's reduction table (Section 05), stack the RMSA impact on top, retiring even a few years earlier can mean a meaningfully smaller medical-premium subsidy for the rest of their pre-Medicare life, not just a smaller pension check.

Section 11

IRMAA & income planning

Once a client reaches Medicare, premiums aren't flat, they rise with income. The Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount (IRMAA) adds a surcharge to Medicare Part B and Part D premiums when modified adjusted gross income (MAGI) crosses a threshold.

FeatureWhat it means for planning
Two-year lookback2026 premiums are set using 2024 MAGI. Income today shows up in premiums about two years later.
A cliff, not a rampCrossing a threshold by even $1 triggers the full surcharge for that tier, there's no gradual phase-in.
Per personFor a married couple, each Medicare-enrolled spouse pays the surcharge once joint MAGI crosses a tier.

For reference, 2026 IRMAA begins above $109,000 MAGI (single) and $218,000 (married filing jointly), on a five-tier scale topping out at $500,000 single / $750,000 MFJ. These thresholds change yearly, always pull the current figures: medicare.gov: Medicare costs & IRMAA or ssa.gov: Medicare premiums.

What feeds a PG&E client's MAGI

  • Final Pay or Cash Balance annuity income — counts as ordinary income
  • Cash Balance lump-sum rollovers — no immediate tax if rolled directly to an IRA, but later withdrawals count
  • 401(k)/RSP withdrawals and Roth conversions — ordinary income in the year taken
  • RMSA reimbursements — generally not taxable and do not add to MAGI, useful to know since it means using the RMSA doesn't cost a client IRMAA-wise
Client example: Maria Delgado

Maria's Final Pay Pension pays $3,680.13/month (Section 08) once she elects the joint pension, $44,161.56/year. On its own, that's well under the $109,000 single-filer IRMAA threshold. But suppose that same year she also does a $70,000 Roth conversion to use up her low tax bracket: her MAGI jumps to $114,161.56, just $5,161.56 over the line. Because IRMAA is a cliff, that modest overage triggers the full first-tier surcharge on both Part B and Part D, for two years, not a partial or prorated amount. A $60,000 conversion instead would have kept her under the threshold entirely.

Sizing a Roth conversion

The gap between retiring and the start of Social Security and Required Minimum Distributions is often a client's lowest-income stretch, especially for someone who retires early with a reduced or delayed pension. The disciplined approach is to "fill the bracket": convert just enough pre-tax money to Roth to use up a tax bracket without spilling into the next one or crossing an IRMAA tier two years out.

Not advice: a framework

Conversion and withdrawal sequencing depend on a client's full tax picture and goals. Use this as a framework to evaluate with the client and their tax professional, not a default recommendation.

Section 12 · Advisor Toolkit

Strategy playbook

The recurring, high-value decisions where a PG&E union client benefits most from advice.

Identify the pension formula

Hire date (before/after Jan 1, 2013) and any 2013 election decide whether the client has a Final Pay Pension, a Cash Balance Pension, or both.

Check the 30-year exemption

For Final Pay clients, 30+ years of service means a fully unreduced pension at any age 55–65. This is the highest-leverage number to check first.

Model the early-retirement trade-off

Use the reduction table (Section 05) to show exactly what retiring at 55, 58, or 62 costs versus waiting, in dollars, not just percentages.

Choose the survivor structure deliberately

Single Life vs. Joint Pension (25/50/75/100%) vs. the Pop-Up option is a permanent choice for married clients and needs spousal consent.

Confirm the 401(k) match is fully captured

Final Pay participants need 6% of pay contributed (after 3 years) to get the full 60% match; Cash Balance participants need 8% to get the full $0.75-per-dollar match.

Project the RMSA balance

Estimate the account using the $5,000/year-from-45 formula and stack it against the pension reduction to show the full cost of retiring early, not just the smaller pension check.

Sequence income around IRMAA

Coordinate pension start date, RMSA usage, 401(k) withdrawals, and Social Security claiming to manage Medicare premium surcharges and tax brackets.

Use direct rollovers

Any lump sum or 401(k) distribution should move via direct rollover to preserve tax deferral and avoid the mandatory 20% federal withholding on cash payouts.

First-meeting question set

What year were you hired at PG&E, and were you employed on 12/31/2012? Did you make an election during the 2013 Cash Balance choice window? Current age and years of service? Target retirement age? Married, and what's your spouse's own coverage and income? What other savings and Social Security expectations do you have? These answers drive every recommendation above.

Client example: Maria Delgado, the full picture

Putting it together: Maria (hired 2008, Final Pay Pension) is deciding between retiring at 58 with 22 years ($2,607.18/month, Section 05) or 65 with 28 years ($3,885.96/month, Section 04). Waiting also grows her RMSA past the $92,500 base (Section 10) and avoids the 401(k) match gap if she keeps contributing 6% ($3,960/year, Section 09). If she does retire at 65 and elects the 50% joint pension, she and her husband land at $3,680.13/month with survivor protection (Section 08), and a same-year Roth conversion above roughly $60,000 would push her into an IRMAA surcharge (Section 11). That's five separate decisions, hire date, retirement age, survivor election, 401(k) rate, and conversion sizing, that only make sense evaluated together.

Section 13

Protections & rules to know

PBGC insurance

The pension is federally insured by the Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation. If the plan ever terminated underfunded, PBGC would step in to pay benefits up to legal limits. Non-pension benefits (like the RMSA) are not PBGC-insured.

QDRO (divorce)

Under California community-property law, pension benefits earned during marriage can be divided in divorce, but only through a court-issued Qualified Domestic Relations Order (QDRO). Benefits otherwise can't be assigned, pledged, or reached by creditors. Example: if Devon (10 years of service, all of it while married) divorces, his ex-spouse's share would generally be based on the community-property portion of his Cash Balance account earned during the marriage, PG&E won't apply any split without a filed QDRO on record.

Transferring between union and management roles

A client who moves between a union-represented position and a Management/A&T role has their pension calculated as the larger of: (a) all service under the formula for their current role, or (b) the union-classification benefit plus the management-classification benefit, each calculated under its own rules.

Survivor benefits before retirement

If an employee dies before starting pension payments, a spouse or named beneficiary generally receives a Pre-Retirement Survivor's Pension, an ongoing monthly benefit, calculated differently depending on the employee's age and service at death.

Funding-level limits

Federal law can restrict lump-sum payments if the plan's funded status drops below 80%, and can freeze future accruals if it drops below 60%. PG&E must notify participants if either restriction applies.

No guarantee of employment

Participating in the Retirement Plan does not create any right to continued employment with PG&E.

Section 14

Glossary

Final Pay Pension
The older pension formula for employees hired before 2013: pay near retirement × a service-based percentage.
Cash Balance Pension
The current pension formula: a hypothetical account that grows via annual pay credits and quarterly interest credits.
Points
Age plus years of service (whole numbers), used to determine the Cash Balance annual pay credit percentage.
Pay credit
The annual percentage of covered pay added to a Cash Balance account, 5% to 10% depending on points.
Interest credit
Quarterly interest added to a Cash Balance account, based on the average 30-year Treasury bond yield.
Basic Weekly Pay
Straight-time hourly rate × 2,080 hours ÷ 52 weeks, rounded up to the nearest $10; the pay figure used in the Final Pay formula.
Credited service
Years of employment counted toward vesting and benefit calculations under the Retirement Plan.
Vesting
The point a pension benefit becomes the employee's to keep even if they leave PG&E (3 years Cash Balance / 5 years Final Pay, or age 55).
Early retirement reduction
The percentage cut applied to a Final Pay Pension for starting payments before age 65, unless the employee has 30+ years of service.
Single Life Pension
A monthly pension paid for the retiree's lifetime only, with nothing continuing to a survivor.
Joint Pension
A reduced monthly pension that continues (at 25%, 50%, 75%, or 100%) to a named joint pensioner after the retiree's death.
Special Joint Pension ("Pop-Up")
A joint pension that returns to the full single-life amount if the joint pensioner dies before the retiree.
RMSA
Retiree Medical Savings Account. A PG&E-funded account that reimburses a percentage of retiree medical premiums; no cash value.
Roth 401(k) contribution
An after-tax contribution source added to the RSP effective Jan 1, 2026; qualified growth and withdrawals come out tax-free. Distinct from the "regular after-tax" bucket, where only the contributions (not the earnings) are tax-free.
Spillover election
A 401(k) feature that automatically shifts contributions from pre-tax to after-tax once the annual pre-tax limit is reached, so employer match can continue.
Excess Plan
A PG&E-paid, non-qualified benefit that pays (as a lump sum at retirement) the portion of a pension that exceeds IRS §415/§401(a)(17) limits. Unsecured; not PBGC-insured.
PBGC
Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation, the federal agency that insures pension benefits.
QDRO
Qualified Domestic Relations Order, a court order that can divide pension benefits in a divorce.
SPD
Summary Plan Description, the plain-language document describing a benefit plan's official terms.
402(g) limit
The annual IRS cap on an employee's own 401(k) deferrals. $24,500 in 2026.
415(c) limit
The annual IRS cap on all money added to a 401(k) account combined (employee + employer). $72,000 in 2026.
Catch-up contribution
Extra 401(k) deferrals allowed at age 50+, with a larger "super catch-up" for ages 60–63.
IRMAA
Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount, an income-based surcharge on Medicare Part B and D premiums.
MAGI
Modified adjusted gross income, the measure that determines IRMAA tiers.
